Question 3: Can a person be arrested without being informed of the grounds?
Answer: In general, the answer is no, a person cannot be arrested without being informed of the grounds for their arrest. This is a fundamental principle of due process and is protected by various laws and constitutions around the world.

In the United States, for example, the Fourth Amendment to the Constitution requires that arrests be made with probable cause and that the person being arrested be informed of the charges against them. This is often referred to as the " Miranda warning" or "Miranda rights," which includes the right to remain silent, the right to an attorney, and the right to be informed of the charges.

Similarly, in other countries, such as the United Kingdom, Canada, and Australia, the law requires that a person be informed of the reason for their arrest and the charges against them at the time of arrest or as soon as practicable thereafter.

However, there are some exceptions to this general rule. For example:

1. Exigent circumstances: In emergency situations, such as when a person is fleeing from the police or poses an immediate threat to themselves or others, an arrest can be made without immediately informing the person of the grounds.
2. National security: In cases involving national security, such as terrorism or espionage, the police may be authorized to arrest a person without disclosing the full grounds for the arrest.
3. Warrantless arrests: In some jurisdictions, police officers may be authorized to make warrantless arrests in certain circumstances, such as when a crime is committed in their presence. In these cases, the person may not be immediately informed of the grounds for the arrest.

It's worth noting that even in cases where an arrest is made without immediate disclosure of the grounds, the person is still entitled to be informed of the charges against them as soon as practicable, and to have access to legal counsel and other due process protections.

Article 25-A of the Constitution states that "the State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of five to sixteen years in such manner as may be determined by law."
